And it’s not just the code. It’s not just the documentation either (which, if I’m being honest, in my projects usually exists on some higher spiritual plane. Sorry about that {{ dmoji(name="xdd") }}). There’s also everything else: managing a community, answering questions, helping people who decided to use your project… or even contribute to it.

And that… is hard.

It’s one of those things nobody teaches you. There’s no tutorial like *“How to manage humans in open source projects for dummies.”* But you publish something, people start using it, and suddenly you’re learning everything on the fly.

Not just for the good of the project, but because of some kind of… sense of honor, I guess.

I don’t know.

The truth is, I feel pretty guilty when someone asks me something and I don’t have the answer. Or when I don’t have the time to help. Or, even worse, when I simply don’t know how to do it.

*And I shouldn’t feel that way.*

There’s also the other side of the coin: the people who seem to believe you owe them something.

That you must fix that bug that annoys them.
That you must implement that extremely specific feature only they need.
That you must do it now.

Just because.

It’s a strange feeling.

Because at the end of the day, my projects are open source. I make them purely out of passion. For free. Nobody pays me for it and, being completely honest, I have absolutely no obligation to do anything.

I could literally delete the repository tomorrow, abandon it completely, disappear into the woods… and no one would really have the right to complain about it.

It’s my project.

I do it because it’s fun, not because I have a boss.

And the ironic thing is that, very often, the people who complain the most, demand the most, and expect the most… are also the ones who contribute the least.

Curious, isn’t it?

They want a free project, but they also want to be treated like paying customers with priority support, a contract, and probably coffee included.

Speaking of [coffee](https://ko-fi.com/Axenide), donations are another interesting topic. The people who have donated to me so far are, interestingly enough, the ones who ask me for the least. And that always catches my attention.

My friend [Brys](https://github.com/brys0), for example, has donated several times. But he’s never asked for anything in return. Not a feature, not priority, nothing.

It’s as if he perfectly understands that the project exists because I enjoy making it, not because I have any obligation to anyone.

And I appreciate that a lot.

If you look at the ratio between people who contribute and people who just consume, it’s probably something like 99 to 1. My projects are small, but in larger projects that ratio is probably even more extreme.

And that’s fine. I never expected people to come and contribute code. Not even documentation (again: that mythical creature {{ dmoji(name="xdd") }}).

But when part of that 99% starts demanding things as if someone had promised them something… it honestly becomes a bit frustrating.

Especially when they seem to assume the project is the only thing I do.

Buddy.

I have a life.
I have to study.
I’m broke.
I don’t have much time.

And yet, every time I get a bit of free time, the first thing I often do is go work on the project.

Do you know why?

**Because it’s fun.**
